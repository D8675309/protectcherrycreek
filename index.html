The Case Against VA-2026-02

City records, the developer's own documents, and the Planning Director's own words — in 4 minutes.

Federal Wildlife Law

Bald Eagles Are Nesting in Our Neighborhood

At least four bald eagle nests have been observed surrounding the Cherry Creek / Lake Laurie area. Under federal law, this has direct consequences for any nearby construction or rezoning — and the developer has filed zero environmental studies.

No environmental survey has been filed with the VA-2026-02 rezoning application. The presence of active bald eagle nests within the project area is a significant legal and procedural gap that the Planning Commission cannot responsibly overlook.

Three Overlapping Federal Protections

Since 1940

Bald & Golden Eagle Protection Act

Prohibits taking, disturbing, or harming eagles, their nests, or eggs. Covers construction-related disturbance even when eagles are not present, including off-season construction near a previously used nest site.

Since 1918

Migratory Bird Treaty Act

Provides an additional layer of federal protection for bald eagles as migratory birds. Violations are federal criminal offenses independent of the Eagle Act.

Delisted 2007

Still Fully Protected

Though removed from the ESA list in 2007 due to successful recovery, bald eagles retain full protection under BGEPA and MBTA. Georgia DNR enforces all three laws in coordination with federal USFWS.

Mandatory Construction Buffer Zones

U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service guidelines require minimum distances between construction activity and bald eagle nests.

Distance Trigger Condition Relevance to This Site
660 ft Construction visible from the nest Applies to all 8 proposed 4-story buildings, which would be highly visible across the open lake and field terrain.
330 ft Minimum if nest is visually screened The open-water character of this site offers little natural screening, likely triggering the larger 660-ft requirement.
Feb 1 – Jul 15 Active nesting season Buffer restrictions are in full effect throughout this window. The March 30 hearing falls within this season.
½ mile Tree retention zone near water USFWS best practices require protecting mature trees within half a mile of water. The proposed site falls within this zone.

Why This Matters for the Rezoning Decision

1
No USFWS consultation has occurred.

Before any construction can legally proceed near active eagle nests, the developer must contact the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service. This has not been done.

2
The 660-foot buffer likely overlaps proposed buildings.

With Lake Laurie providing open sight lines and four known nest locations surrounding the property, federal buffer requirements almost certainly encumber portions of the proposed site plan.

3
Georgia DNR has been formally alerted.

A resident's inquiry to the DNR Wildlife Resources Division has created an official record. DNR confirmation of nest sites would give the Planning Commission grounds to require a wildlife survey before any vote.

4
Approval now creates liability exposure.

If rezoning is granted without addressing the eagle issue, subsequent construction could result in federal violations under the BGEPA — with civil penalties up to $5,000 per incident and potential criminal liability.

$5,000 Civil penalty per violation
$10,000 Criminal fine (repeat)
1 Year Federal imprisonment
3 Laws Federal statutes protecting eagles

Demand an Eagle Survey Before Any Vote

At the March 30 Planning Commission hearing and the April 9 City Council meeting, formally request that USFWS consultation and a certified bald eagle survey be required as a condition of any rezoning approval. No development application that ignores the presence of federally protected wildlife should be permitted to proceed.
